JURISDICTION: New York
COURT: Court of Appeals
YEAR: 1985

In determining an equitable distribution of marital property, the court must consider the contributions of each spouse to the marriage and to the acquisition of marital assets. During the course of the marriage, the wife made substantial direct and indirect contributions to the husband’s attainment of a medical license. She provided financial support, managed the household, and made personal sacrifices that enabled the husband to complete his professional education and training.

Although a professional license is not tangible property, the enhanced earning capacity derived from the license was developed during the marriage and is the product of the joint efforts of both spouses. The court therefore considers the wife’s contributions to the development of that earning capacity in fashioning an equitable distribution. The evidence demonstrates that the wife’s role was essential to the husband’s professional advancement and future economic prospects.

The duration of the marriage further supports recognition of the wife’s substantial contributions. The parties were married throughout the period during which the husband pursued and obtained his medical degree and license. The court finds that equity requires acknowledgment of the non-titled spouse’s contributions to the other spouse’s career and earning potential when determining a fair distributive award.

In light of these considerations, the court places significant weight on the wife’s contributions to the husband’s enhanced earning capacity in reaching its equitable distribution determination.
